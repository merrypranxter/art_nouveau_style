# Art Nouveau: Notable Works

## Overview

This document catalogues the canonical masterworks of Art Nouveau across all media — poster art, painting, glass, jewellery, furniture, and applied arts. Each entry includes production details, formal analysis, and notes on the work's significance within the movement's history.

---

## Alphonse Mucha (1860–1939)

### *Gismonda* (1894)
**Medium:** Colour lithograph  
**Dimensions:** 216 × 74.2 cm  
**Client:** Sarah Bernhardt / Théâtre de la Renaissance, Paris  
**Current location:** Multiple copies; major holdings at the Mucha Museum, Prague, and the Museum of Decorative Arts, Prague

Mucha received this commission in late December 1894, when Bernhardt's regular poster artist was unavailable. The resulting work — produced in days under extreme pressure — is one of the defining images of Art Nouveau.

The composition centres on Bernhardt in full costume as the Byzantine princess Gismonda: a tall, narrow figure (the format is unusually long and narrow, approximately 2.15 metres in height) in elaborate Byzantine dress, holding a palm frond. Above her, a pointed arch of Byzantine mosaic frames her head; below, a band of acanthus-and-flower ornament carries the title text. The border is exceptionally elegant — bands of stylised flowers alternating with geometric patterns, derived from Byzantine and Romanesque ornament rather than from the sinuous botanical Art Nouveau that would characterise Mucha's later work.

The colour palette is characteristic of his mature style: warm cream, muted gold, soft grey-green, and the dusty rose of the floral border. The figure's robe is rendered in complex overlapping tones that suggest the richness of Byzantine embroidery without imitating it literally.

**Significance:** The poster made Mucha's name overnight. Bernhardt, upon seeing the finished poster, immediately contracted him for six further productions. The format — tall and narrow, with the figure extending nearly the full height of the composition, with text and ornament at top and bottom — became the template for all of Mucha's subsequent theatrical posters.

### *Job* Cigarette Papers (1896)
**Medium:** Colour lithograph  
**Dimensions:** 67 × 47 cm (standard format); also produced in smaller billboard and package formats  
**Client:** Chambon Frères / Job brand

The *Job* poster represents Mucha at his most purely iconic. A young woman's ecstatic upturned face is surrounded by swirling curls of smoke-like hair and the sinuous paper of a rolled cigarette, both rendered as a single unified organic line system. The expression — eyes partly closed, lips parted, expression ambiguously suggesting pleasure, reverie, or arousal — is one of the most perfectly calculated images of the Art Nouveau feminine ideal.

The composition is unusually intimate for a commercial poster: the figure fills the picture plane, and the ornamental elements (a circular cartouche above, the brand name in stylised Art Nouveau lettering below) are secondary to the relationship between the viewer and the figure.

**Colour:** The palette is among Mucha's most refined — warm cream ground, the soft gold of the hair, a muted sage green in the background, and the warm ivory of the figure's skin.

**Significance:** The *Job* poster became one of the most widely reproduced images of the Art Nouveau era, and its intimate format (also reproduced at small scale on cigarette paper packaging) made it unusually pervasive in everyday visual culture.

### *The Seasons* — First Series (1896)
**Medium:** Colour lithograph (four panels)  
**Dimensions:** Each approximately 103 × 54 cm  
**Publisher:** F. Champenois, Paris

Mucha's first *Seasons* series established the format that would define his decorative panel work: four female allegories, each representing a season through costume, botanical surround, and expression, presented as a set of identical-format panels to be displayed together or separately.

**Spring:** A young woman in white, surrounded by flowering branches and butterflies, with an expression of innocent joy. The palette is cool and pale — white, pale green, pink blossom.

**Summer:** A figure in richer drapery, surrounded by full-bloomed roses and summer foliage. The palette is warmer and more saturated — rose, deep green, warm gold.

**Autumn:** A figure with more complex expression, surrounded by harvest fruits and turning foliage. The ochre and russet tones of the palette carry a melancholy undertone.

**Winter:** The most striking of the four: a heavily cloaked figure, her face partially buried in her mantle, surrounded by bare branches. The palette is the coolest and most restrained — grey-white, icy blue, deep charcoal.

**Significance:** The *Seasons* panels created the template for Art Nouveau decorative print production — the allegorical female figure, the botanical surround, the unified multi-panel set — that would be imitated by dozens of designers and printers throughout Europe for the following decade.

### *Monaco-Monte Carlo* (1897)
**Medium:** Colour lithograph  
**Dimensions:** 110 × 76 cm  
**Client:** Paris-Lyon-Méditerranée Railway

Mucha's travel poster for the Principality of Monaco demonstrates the movement of his style into landscape and place-identity representation. The composition combines his characteristic central female figure with a background of stylised Mediterranean landscape — olive branches, the distant blue of the Ligurian Sea, the terracotta of Mediterranean architecture.

The title text is integrated into the lower third of the composition, within a cartouche of olive branches. The palette shifts from the typical warm cream toward the cool blues and greens of the Mediterranean setting.

---

### *Sarah Bernhardt Theatrical Series* (1894–1900)
**Medium:** Colour lithographs  
**Client:** Various Paris theatres; produced by Champenois

Beyond *Gismonda*, Mucha designed a series of major theatrical posters for Bernhardt:

**La Dame aux Camélias (1896):** Bernhardt as Marguerite Gautier, in a composition of extraordinary melancholy beauty. The figure is surrounded by camellias — the titular flower — in a palette of soft pink and dusty grey.

**Lorenzaccio (1896):** A more dramatic, dark composition with strong diagonal movement.

**Médée (1898):** Perhaps the most disturbing of the series: Bernhardt as the murderous Medea, standing over the bodies of her children. The composition retains Mucha's characteristic decorative elements but carries a genuine dramatic weight.

**La Tosca (1898):** Strong diagonal composition, with architectural elements.

**Hamlet (1899):** A more austere and dramatic work, with unusual colour restraint.

### *The Slav Epic* (1910–1928)
**Medium:** Egg tempera and oil on canvas (20 panels)  
**Dimensions:** Each panel approximately 4.1 × 4.8 metres to 6.2 × 8.1 metres (variable)  
**Current location:** City of Prague (on long-term loan to the Veletržní palác)

Mucha's late masterwork is unlike anything else in his career and indeed in Art Nouveau as a whole. Twenty enormous paintings depicting the history of the Slavic peoples — from their mythological origins through the Hussite wars to the nineteenth-century national awakening — constitute an astonishing late achievement of ambition and technical power.

The *Slav Epic* is not conventionally Art Nouveau: the large-scale figurative painting in egg tempera follows academic tradition more than poster practice. But the visual vocabulary — the treatment of hair and drapery, the decorative figures at the margins, the compositional integration of architecture and figure — shows the unmistakable influence of Mucha's decorative work throughout.

## Gustav Klimt (1862–1918)

### *The Kiss* (1907–1908)
**Medium:** Oil paint and gold leaf on canvas  
**Dimensions:** 180 × 180 cm  
**Current location:** Österreichische Galerie Belvedere, Vienna

The single most universally reproduced work of the Art Nouveau movement. A man and woman embrace at the edge of a flower-covered cliff; their figures are enveloped in a single unified garment of extraordinary ornamental complexity — gold-leaf rectangles and circles covering the male figure, gold-leaf circles and flowers covering the female.

The composition achieves something that few works in the history of decorative art have managed: the complete unification of figurative meaning and abstract decoration. The embrace of the two figures and the abstract ornamental surface of their combined garment are not illustrative content with ornament added; they are a single aesthetic fact. The figures barely exist as anatomical entities — they are visible in the faces, hands, and feet, and everywhere else they become pure pattern.

**Technique:** Klimt applied gold leaf directly to the canvas surface alongside oil paint. The resulting surface is simultaneously painting and relief object.

---

### *Judith I* (1901)
**Medium:** Oil paint and gold leaf on canvas  
**Dimensions:** 84 × 42 cm  
**Current location:** Österreichische Galerie Belvedere, Vienna

Judith stands with Holofernes' severed head, her expression neither triumph nor horror but something far more ambiguous — a sensual, self-absorbed satisfaction that is quintessentially Symbolist in its suggestion of female erotic power. The upper portion of the painting is dominated by a gold-leaf landscape of extraordinary abstract beauty; the actual subject — the head of Holofernes — appears as a small detail at the lower edge.

---

### *Portrait of Adele Bloch-Bauer I* (1907)
**Medium:** Oil paint and gold leaf on canvas  
**Dimensions:** 138 × 138 cm  
**Current location:** Neue Galerie, New York (since 2006)

Known as "The Woman in Gold," this portrait carries the Secessionist Gesamtkunstwerk ideal to its logical extreme: the sitter is almost entirely absorbed into her ornamental setting. The figure, dress, chair, and background are merged into a single golden field from which only the face and hands emerge as clearly delineated forms. Egyptian eyes, Byzantine spirals, and abstract geometric patterns in gold leaf create a surface of dazzling complexity.

## Aubrey Beardsley (1872–1898)

### *Salome* Illustrations (1894)
**Medium:** Pen and ink  
**Publisher:** John Lane at the Bodley Head, London

Beardsley's sixteen illustrations for Oscar Wilde's *Salome* — some accepted for publication, some censored — are among the most technically accomplished and aesthetically disturbing works in the Art Nouveau tradition.

Working in pure black and white, Beardsley achieved effects through the precision of his pen line that colour lithographers achieved with multiple printings. The flat areas of black, the meticulous patterning of decorative backgrounds, the peacock feather motifs, and the grotesque figures of Salome and her court demonstrate the Japanese influence on his work while creating something entirely his own.

**Notable images:** "The Woman in the Moon" (rejected; shows Wilde's face in the moon); "The Toilet of Salome" (two versions, the second milder); "The Climax" (Salome kisses Jokanaan's severed head); "J'ai baisé ta bouche, Iokanaan."

---

### *Yellow Book* Covers (1894–1895)
**Medium:** Pen and ink (reproduced in letterpress)

Beardsley served as art editor for the first four issues of *The Yellow Book*, a quarterly literary magazine that became the defining publication of the 1890s Aesthetic movement. His covers — alternately shocking and beautiful, consistently pushing the limits of what Victorian publication design would tolerate — made the journal one of the most discussed cultural objects in Britain.

## Henri de Toulouse-Lautrec (1864–1901)

### *Moulin Rouge: La Goulue* (1891)
**Medium:** Colour lithograph  
**Dimensions:** 191 × 117 cm  
**Client:** Moulin Rouge, Paris

Toulouse-Lautrec's first major poster created the template for Art Nouveau poster design in France and established him overnight as a major figure. The composition is extraordinary in its reduction: the dancer La Goulue in the centre distance is rendered as a pale silhouette; in the foreground, the dancer Valentin le Désossé is a black shadow with a distinctive top hat; the crowd around the dance floor is suggested by an impressionistic mass of black bowler hats.

The poster demonstrates the Japanese compositional influence at its most direct: the radical cropping, the tonal simplification (only three colour areas in the primary composition), and the integration of a strong black silhouette with a white background.

---

### *Jane Avril* Series (1893–1899)
**Medium:** Colour lithograph (multiple works)

Toulouse-Lautrec made four major posters of the dancer Jane Avril, each a masterpiece of characterisation and technical invention. The 1893 *Jardin de Paris* poster, with its extraordinary double-bass neck framing the composition as an ornamental device, is particularly celebrated.

## Émile Gallé (1846–1904) — Glass

### Cameo Glass Vases with Iris Motifs (c. 1895–1904)
**Medium:** Cameo glass (acid-etched and wheel-carved)  
**Current location:** Various museums (Musée d'Orsay, Victoria and Albert Museum, Corning Museum of Glass)

Gallé produced an extensive series of cameo glass pieces with iris subjects — the tall, structurally complex iris flower providing ideal material for the multiple-layer acid-etching technique. The deep purple-blue of the iris petals, rendered in a heavily cased glass layer above a paler ground, achieves a depth and richness that painting on glass could never match.

### Dragonfly Vases (c. 1897–1904)
**Medium:** Cameo glass with marqueterie de verre  
**Current location:** Various

Among Gallé's most technically ambitious works, using his distinctive *marqueterie de verre* technique to create surfaces in which the dragonfly forms appear almost to be embedded within the glass rather than applied to its surface.

## Louis Comfort Tiffany (1848–1933)

### *Wisteria* Lamp (1902)
**Medium:** Leaded Favrile glass; patinated bronze base  
**Current location:** Multiple examples; fine examples at the Metropolitan Museum of Art, New York, and the Corning Museum of Glass

The *Wisteria* lamp is perhaps the most technically complex object produced by any Art Nouveau workshop. The shade contains over 2,000 individually shaped pieces of glass in approximately eighteen colours, assembled using the copper-foil technique. The wisteria blossoms hang in dense clusters from a framework of curving vine branches rendered in three-dimensional cast bronze; the transition from the bronze vine framework of the shade to the gnarled, naturalistic bronze base is the most complete fusion of structure and ornament in American Arts and Crafts.

---

### Dragonfly Lamp (c. 1900–1906)
**Medium:** Leaded Favrile glass; mosaic glass elements; bronze

Among Tiffany's most beloved designs, the Dragonfly lamp features large dragonfly forms — with wings of radiating leaded glass — arranged around the shade as the primary compositional element. Millefiori glass inserts create the dragonfly's jewel-like eyes.
