# Art Nouveau: Primary Color Palettes

## Overview

Color in Art Nouveau is not incidental decoration but a primary vehicle of meaning and atmosphere. The movement's characteristic palettes were shaped by the limitations of chromolithographic printing, by the philosophy of organic naturalism, and by the specific aesthetic sensibilities of its regional variants. This document catalogues the four principal regional palettes with specific hex codes, names, and usage notes.

---

## Palette 1: Mucha Poster Palette

The palette most closely associated with Alphonse Mucha's commercial poster work — developed in close collaboration with the Champenois printing house in Paris — is characterised by muted, slightly chalky tones rather than saturated primaries. The underlying influence is the dry, slightly faded quality of fresco painting and the earthy tones of Byzantine mosaic.

### Core Palette

| Name | Hex Code | RGB | Usage Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Warm Cream | `#F5E6C8` | 245, 230, 200 | Primary background; the lightest tone in most compositions; skin highlight areas |
| Antique Gold | `#C9A84C` | 201, 168, 76 | Halos, borders, metallic accents, hair highlights; the single most characteristic Mucha colour |
| Dusty Rose | `#D4A0A0` | 212, 160, 160 | Drapery, flower petals, cheek tones; the characteristic "Mucha pink" |
| Muted Sage | `#8A9E78` | 138, 158, 120 | Background botanical areas, mid-ground foliage; never fully saturated |
| Warm Terracotta | `#B5654A` | 181, 101, 74 | Accent colour; architectural elements; warm shadow areas |
| Pale Lilac | `#C4B0D0` | 196, 176, 208 | Sky areas, background gradients, cool drapery |
| Olive Drab | `#6B7040` | 107, 112, 64 | Deep foliage, stem elements, lower border areas |
| Ivory White | `#FAF0DC` | 250, 240, 220 | Lightest skin tones, flower centres, text fields |
| Deep Amber | `#9C6B20` | 156, 107, 32 | Deep gold accents, letter outlines, heavy border elements |
| Dusty Mauve | `#9E7E8A` | 158, 126, 138 | Shadow areas on skin and drapery, subtle background tones |

### Palette Characteristics

The Mucha poster palette operates on two principles:

1. **High-key warmth:** The overall tonality tends toward warm, slightly yellowed neutrals — the warm cream background, the antique gold accents, the terracotta shadows — that create an impression of firelight or late afternoon sun. This warmth is never harsh; the chalkiness of the tones ensures they read as aged, refined, and slightly dreamlike.

2. **Botanical saturation control:** The greens and botanical tones are always desaturated and grayed rather than pure or vivid. A pure emerald or grass green would compete with the figure and disturb the harmony; Mucha's greens are always slightly dirty, slightly grey, tending toward olive.

### When to Use

Use the Mucha poster palette for: allegorical female figure compositions, decorative panel work, poster-style compositions with central human figures, any work requiring the characteristic Art Nouveau "golden warmth."

## Palette 2: Vienna Secession Palette

The Vienna Secession's palette is dramatically different from the French mainstream — far more austere, more geometric in its colour blocking, and more clearly influenced by the black-white-gold tradition of Japanese graphic design and the linear austerity of Mackintosh's Glasgow Style.

### Core Palette

| Name | Hex Code | RGB | Usage Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Vienna Black | `#1A1A1A` | 26, 26, 26 | Primary line weight, text, graphic outlines; the most important tone |
| White Ground | `#F8F6F0` | 248, 246, 240 | Background; not pure white but slightly warm off-white |
| Secession Gold | `#C8A840` | 200, 168, 64 | The Secession building's dome colour; gold accents, ornamental highlights |
| Deep Purple | `#4A2060` | 74, 32, 96 | Klimt's characteristic jewel-tone purple; secondary accent |
| Malachite Green | `#3D6B50` | 61, 107, 80 | Ver Sacrum botanical elements; slightly cooler than French greens |
| Pale Gold | `#E8D080` | 232, 208, 128 | Large gold fields, background gilding, warm light areas |
| Crimson | `#8B1A2A` | 139, 26, 42 | Strong accent; Ver Sacrum cover typography; decorative medallions |
| Silver Grey | `#B0B0A8` | 176, 176, 168 | Secondary ground tone; architectural surfaces |
| Ivory | `#F2EDD8` | 242, 237, 216 | Figure ground, text panels |
| Deep Gold-Brown | `#7A5A20` | 122, 90, 32 | Shadow within gold areas; lower register of gilded ornament |

### Palette Characteristics

The Vienna Secession palette is defined by its **high contrast**. The primary visual relationship is black against white/off-white, with gold as the primary accent. Colour is used sparingly and with deliberate intensity: when purple or crimson appears, it reads as a jewel-tone accent within a predominantly achromatic field.

This palette reflects the Secession's intellectual position at the border of Art Nouveau and early modernism: the organic ornamental impulse is present but subjected to a discipline of reduction and geometric order.

### When to Use

Use the Vienna Secession palette for: black-and-white graphic compositions with gold accents, architectural drawings, publication design inspired by *Ver Sacrum*, Klimt-style compositions with gold leaf effects.

## Palette 3: Glasgow Style Palette

The Glasgow Style palette — associated with Charles Rennie Mackintosh, Margaret Macdonald, and the Four — is among the most distinctive in the Art Nouveau family. It is muted, cool, and feminine in a way that is quite different from the warm honey tones of the French mainstream or the high-contrast drama of the Secession.

### Core Palette

| Name | Hex Code | RGB | Usage Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Mackintosh Lilac | `#B8A8C8` | 184, 168, 200 | The single most characteristic Glasgow colour; appears in furniture, textiles, interiors |
| Silver Rose | `#D4B0B0` | 212, 176, 176 | Rose pink — the Glasgow rose in its characteristic muted form |
| Pewter Grey | `#8A8A8A` | 138, 138, 138 | Structural tone; stone surfaces, architectural elements |
| Cream White | `#F0EDE0` | 240, 237, 224 | Background and ground; slightly cooler than Mucha's cream |
| Moss Green | `#6A7A58` | 106, 122, 88 | Foliage; the Glasgow botanical palette, cooler and greyer than Nancy |
| Charcoal | `#2A2A2A` | 42, 42, 42 | Heavy line and structural elements; not as absolute as Vienna black |
| Soft Sage | `#A8B898` | 168, 184, 152 | Secondary foliage; lighter botanical elements |
| Dusty Pink | `#C8909A` | 200, 144, 154 | Rose accents; slightly deeper than silver rose |
| Stone Beige | `#C8BC9E` | 200, 188, 158 | Interior stone; background mid-tone |
| Deep Plum | `#602850` | 96, 40, 80 | Darkest accent; appears in Mackintosh stained glass |

### Palette Characteristics

The Glasgow palette is the quietest and most interior of the Art Nouveau regional palettes. The lilac-rose-grey combination is almost without precedent in the decorative arts and gives the Glasgow Style its immediately recognisable atmosphere of subdued, slightly melancholy beauty.

The Glasgow palette reads as **cool** where Mucha reads as warm — grey rather than gold is the underlying neutral — and **restrained** where the French mainstream reads as richly decorative.

### When to Use

Use the Glasgow Style palette for: interior design concepts, typography and book design, works featuring the Glasgow rose motif, any composition requiring the aesthetic of refined understatement.

## Palette 4: Jugendstil / Nancy School Palette

The German Jugendstil and the French Nancy School share a palette rooted in the natural colours of the northern European landscape — earthy ochres, forest greens, warm browns — with a characteristic richness that reflects the deep, luminous quality of stained glass and enamel work.

### Core Palette

|---|---|---|---|
| Forest Green | `#3A5A38` | 58, 90, 56 | Deep botanical ground; Nancy glass backgrounds |
| Amber Ochre | `#C08830` | 192, 136, 48 | The Jugendstil gold; slightly more orange and earthy than Mucha's antique gold |
| Bark Brown | `#6A4228` | 106, 66, 40 | Structural tree forms, furniture tones, heavy ornamental elements |
| Meadow Yellow | `#D4B848` | 212, 184, 72 | Sunflower tones; Jugendstil's characteristic warm yellow |
| Copper | `#A0522A` | 160, 82, 42 | Nancy metalwork accent; deep warm brown-orange |
| Deep Teal | `#2A5858` | 42, 88, 88 | Nancy glass backgrounds; cool water and botanical tones |
| Sand | `#D4C090` | 212, 192, 144 | Light background; the sandy ground of Munich illustration |
| Rust | `#8A3A20` | 138, 58, 32 | Deep red-brown accent; dried botanical forms |
| Pale Celadon | `#A8C0A0` | 168, 192, 160 | Light foliage, spring botanical elements |
| Walnut | `#4A3018` | 74, 48, 24 | Darkest tone; heavy outline, Nancy furniture wood tones |

### Palette Characteristics

The Jugendstil / Nancy palette is the most **terrestrial** of the Art Nouveau palettes — rooted in the colours of soil, bark, autumn leaves, and forest undergrowth. It has more warmth and more earthy richness than the Glasgow palette, and more darkness and depth than the Mucha poster palette.

In Nancy glass work, these colours are used with the luminosity of transmitted light, so that what appears muddy in pigment becomes glowing amber, forest green, or warm gold in glass.

### When to Use

Use the Jugendstil palette for: German and Austrian decorative work, Nancy School glass-inspired compositions, botanical studies with natural history references, autumn or winter allegorical themes.

## General Colour Principles Across All Palettes

### Saturation Management

In all Art Nouveau palettes, **saturated primary colours are absent**. There is no pure cadmium red, no cobalt blue, no viridian green. All colours are modified toward grey, brown, or gold — earthed and aged. This is both a technical legacy (the chromolithographic inks achieved their most elegant effects with slightly dirty, muted pigments) and an aesthetic principle (vivid colour would feel cheap and commercial, incompatible with the sense of refined beauty the movement sought to project).

### Gold as Unifying Element

In many Art Nouveau compositions, **gold** serves as the unifying tone across multiple palettes. Whether it appears as antique gold, pale gold, amber ochre, or burnished copper, a warm metallic tone provides the high-value accent that lifts the overall palette without introducing artificial brightness.

### The Skin Tone Problem

None of the standard Art Nouveau palettes include explicit skin tones, because skin in Art Nouveau compositions is almost never portrayed with naturalistic variety. The typical representation is extremely pale — a porcelain or ivory tone from the warm cream / ivory white end of the respective palette — that functions more as a neutral ground for the surrounding botanical colour than as a realistic representation of human pigmentation.
