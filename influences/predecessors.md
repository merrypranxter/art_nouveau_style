# Art Nouveau: Predecessors and Influences

## Overview

Art Nouveau did not emerge from nothing. It was the product of a specific convergence of artistic movements, design reform traditions, cross-cultural encounters, and intellectual currents — all concentrated in the final decades of the nineteenth century. This document traces the principal predecessor traditions that fed into the movement.

---

## 1. Japanese Ukiyo-e Woodblock Prints

### The Japonisme Revolution

The single most important external influence on Art Nouveau — and arguably on the whole of late-nineteenth-century Western art — was the encounter with Japanese visual culture that followed the forced opening of Japan to Western trade after 1853–1854. The term **Japonisme**, coined by the French critic Philippe Burty in 1872, described the widespread adoption of Japanese aesthetic principles by European artists and designers.

The ukiyo-e woodblock print tradition — particularly the work of three artists — provided Art Nouveau with several of its most fundamental visual principles:

### Katsushika Hokusai (1760–1849)

Hokusai's *Thirty-Six Views of Mount Fuji* (1830–1833) and his fifteen-volume *Manga* sketchbook (1814–1878) were among the most influential artistic sources for Art Nouveau designers. Specific lessons from Hokusai:

**The great wave:** Hokusai's image of the hollow wave (*Under the Wave off Kanagawa*, c. 1831) demonstrates the possibility of making a natural phenomenon into an abstract decorative form without losing its physical truth. The foam-tipped curl of the wave is simultaneously the most accurate observation of ocean dynamics and one of the most perfect Art Nouveau forms ever created — fifty years before Art Nouveau existed as a category.

**Line as primary form:** In Hokusai's work, the contour line does not merely describe the edge of a form but constitutes the form itself. The thick-to-thin variation of the line, its speed of movement, its calligraphic confidence — all carry expressive weight that Western art had typically assigned to tonal modelling.

**The sketchbook as system:** The *Manga* — which means "random sketches" or "whimsical drawings" — showed European artists a mode of observation in which every aspect of the natural world (insects, plants, fish, human figures, clouds, waves) was available as a source of decorative form. Gallé and Mucha both used the *Manga* as a reference source.

### Utagawa Hiroshige (1797–1858)

Hiroshige's landscape prints — the *Fifty-Three Stations of the Tōkaidō*, the *One Hundred Famous Views of Edo* — contributed primarily through their compositional innovations:

**Flat colour planes in landscape:** Hiroshige demonstrated that landscape could be rendered as flat, bounded colour areas without chiaroscuro modelling and without loss of spatial depth — if compositional devices (overlapping, colour recession, scale gradation) compensated for the absence of tonal depth.

**Atmospheric flatness:** Rain, mist, and snow in Hiroshige's prints are rendered as repeated parallel lines or flat tonal washes that are simultaneously naturalistic and decorative — the rain is both wet and ornamental.

### Kitagawa Utamaro (1753–1806)

Utamaro's bijin-ga (images of beautiful women) were among the most direct influences on Art Nouveau's treatment of the female figure:

**The female profile:** Utamaro's characteristic partial profiles — the head tilted slightly, the nape of the neck emphasised, the hair elaborately coiffed — were directly adopted by Art Nouveau practitioners, particularly by Toulouse-Lautrec and Beardsley.

**The integration of textile pattern:** In Utamaro's prints, the elaborate patterns of kimono fabric are rendered with the same care and precision as the figure's face and hair, and the relationship between the figure and her garment becomes a primary visual subject. This integration of figure and decorative surface is one of Art Nouveau's central principles.

## 2. William Morris and the Arts and Crafts Movement

### Morris & Co. and the Philosophy of Making

William Morris (1834–1896) was the most important single individual in the reform tradition that produced Art Nouveau. Drawing on the writings of John Ruskin — particularly *The Stones of Venice* (1851–1853) and *The Two Paths* (1859) — Morris argued that:

1. Industrial production had severed the craftsperson from the product of their labour, producing alienation both for the worker and in the object itself
2. Beauty was a social and moral as well as an aesthetic quality — an ugly object was a morally deficient object
3. The model for integrated beauty was the medieval workshop, in which the craftsperson designed and executed an object from beginning to end
4. The decorative arts deserved equal dignity and cultural seriousness as painting and sculpture

Morris & Co. (founded 1861) produced wallpapers, textiles, tiles, stained glass, and book designs whose ornamental vocabulary — derived from English meadow flowers, medieval manuscripts, and Flemish tapestries — established the standard that Art Nouveau would inherit and radicalize.

**Specific contributions to Art Nouveau:**
- The insistence that ornament must grow from and be appropriate to its material support
- The flat, stylised treatment of plant forms against a contrasting ground
- The elevation of the decorative arts to equal status with fine art
- The wallpaper and textile as unified ornamental compositions rather than backgrounds

### The Kelmscott Press (1891–1898)

Morris's private press, founded in his last years, produced books of extraordinary beauty in which typography, ornament, and illustration were unified elements of a total design. The Kelmscott books — particularly the *Kelmscott Chaucer* (1896), illustrated by Edward Burne-Jones — directly influenced Art Nouveau book design and typography in Germany (through the Insel-Verlag tradition) and France (through the Symbolist *livres d'artiste*).

## 3. The Pre-Raphaelite Brotherhood

### Rossetti and the Feminine Ideal

The Pre-Raphaelite Brotherhood, founded in London in 1848 by Dante Gabriel Rossetti, William Holman Hunt, and John Everett Millais, contributed to Art Nouveau primarily through its transformation of the pictorial feminine ideal. Rossetti's women — heavy-lidded, full-lipped, with impossible quantities of auburn or golden hair, draped in medieval robes, surrounded by symbolic flowers and objects — established the template that Art Nouveau would inherit.

Specific Pre-Raphaelite contributions:

**Rossetti:** The female face as the primary vehicle of mystical beauty; the symbolically charged botanical setting; the medieval reference.

**Edward Burne-Jones (1833–1898):** A later associate of the Pre-Raphaelites, Burne-Jones was perhaps more directly influential on Art Nouveau than Rossetti. His elongated, slightly attenuated female figures, his flat, jewel-like colour, and his integration of figure and ornamental setting were directly translated into Art Nouveau visual language — particularly in Jan Toorop's work and in Klimt's early decorative style.

**The Arthurian and mythological subjects:** The Pre-Raphaelites' preference for Arthurian legend, Greek mythology, and medieval romance gave Art Nouveau much of its subject matter — the themes of sleeping beauty, the maiden in the tower, the enchantress with supernatural hair.

## 4. Owen Jones and Design Reform

### *The Grammar of Ornament* (1856)

Owen Jones's encyclopaedic survey of world ornamental traditions — covering Egyptian, Greek, Roman, Byzantine, Moorish, Persian, Indian, Celtic, and many other traditions — was one of the most influential design publications of the nineteenth century. Published in a magnificent chromolithographic folio edition, it provided designers with a vast repertoire of non-Western ornamental vocabularies to draw on.

Jones's key principles — that ornament must express structure; that colour relationships must follow rules of harmony; that all ornament is ultimately abstract — were adopted and adapted by the Arts and Crafts movement and subsequently by Art Nouveau.

## 5. Christopher Dresser and Design Science

### The Organic Grammar of Ornament

Christopher Dresser (1834–1904) was perhaps the most intellectually rigorous of the Victorian design reformers, and his contribution to Art Nouveau's formal vocabulary was unique: he attempted to derive a science of ornament from botany. His *The Art of Decorative Design* (1862) and *Principles of Decorative Design* (1873) analysed the structural logic of plant forms — the branching patterns, the leaf-to-stem ratios, the symmetry principles of flowers — and argued that ornamental design should follow these same structural laws.

Dresser's approach validated and formalised what Art Nouveau practitioners were already doing intuitively: deriving their ornamental vocabulary from close observation of natural structures.

## 6. Symbolist Painting

### The Inner World as Subject

The Symbolist painting movement of the 1880s and early 1890s — including Gustave Moreau, Odilon Redon, Fernand Khnopff, Jan Toorop, Franz von Stuck, and the early Klimt — contributed to Art Nouveau the thematic and psychological dimensions that distinguished the movement from mere surface ornamentalism.

**Mysticism and the interior life:** Symbolism's interest in states of consciousness, dream, and mystical experience gave Art Nouveau its atmosphere of dreamy otherworldliness — the female figure's remote expression, the ambiguity between sleep and waking, the symbolic weight of specific flowers.

**The femme fatale and the femme fragile:** Symbolist painting explored the female figure as simultaneously threatening and beautiful, powerful and passive, natural and supernatural. This ambivalence — resolved differently by different Art Nouveau practitioners — permeates the movement's figurative work.

---

## 7. Celtic Revival

### Knotwork and Spiral Forms

The Celtic Revival of the late nineteenth century — driven partly by nationalist politics in Ireland, Scotland, and Wales, partly by scholarly interest in medieval manuscript illumination — contributed to Art Nouveau the spiral and interlace ornamental forms of Celtic metalwork and manuscript art. The Book of Kells and the Lindisfarne Gospels were widely reproduced and studied.

Celtic knotwork's principle of continuous interlacing lines — form created by a single unbroken thread that weaves under and over itself — has a structural parallel with Art Nouveau's continuous line system, and the Glasgow Style in particular drew on Celtic knotwork motifs.

---

## 8. Rococo Decorative Tradition

### The S-Curve Heritage

Art Nouveau's love of the S-curve, the C-scroll, and the asymmetric organic frame had a specific historical precedent in French Rococo interior decoration of the eighteenth century (particularly Louis XV style). The Rococo interior — with its irregular rocaille ornament, its sinuous profile mouldings, its integration of painting with plasterwork — was one of the few periods in Western history that had previously achieved the organicism that Art Nouveau was seeking.

This connection was consciously recognised by some Art Nouveau practitioners: critics who mocked the movement as "Le Style Rococo" were making an identification that had some formal validity.
