# Art Nouveau: Notable Buildings

## Overview

This document catalogues the canonical masterworks of Art Nouveau architecture, organised by architect, with production details, formal analysis, and notes on historical and current significance. These buildings represent the movement's broadest geographic range — from Brussels and Paris to Barcelona, Vienna, Glasgow, and beyond.

---

## Hector Guimard (1867–1942) — France

### Paris Métro Entrance Edicules (1900–1913)

**Type:** Transit infrastructure  
**Location:** Throughout Paris; surviving originals at Abbesses and Porte Dauphine stations  
**Material:** Cast iron, glass  
**Commissioned by:** Compagnie du Métropolitain de Paris  
**Current status:** Protected historical monuments; the original pale grey-green colour is legally maintained

Guimard received the commission to design the entrance structures for the new Paris Métro in 1899, with the first stations opening for the 1900 Exposition Universelle. He designed three types of entrance:

**The edicule** (covered shelter): The most elaborate type, with a glass and iron canopy supported by organic iron columns. The canopy ribs curve upward and outward like the branches of a tree; the finials are cast in plant-bud forms; the glass panels are held in iron frames with botanical ornament. Only two complete edicules survive (Abbesses and Porte Dauphine).

**The édicule-auvent** (half-covered shelter): A simpler type without full enclosure; several survive.

**The entrée de la couronne** (crown entrance): The most minimal type, consisting of iron railings and a sign arch without shelter; many survive.

The surviving entrances are among the most beloved objects in Paris and have been reproduced worldwide — copies exist in Chicago, Montreal, Mexico City, and numerous other cities.

### Castel Béranger, Paris (1894–1898)

**Type:** Apartment building  
**Location:** 14 rue La Fontaine, Paris 16e  
**Materials:** Stone, brick, ceramic tile, cast iron, wrought iron, glass  
**Current status:** Private apartments; façade classified as historical monument

The Castel Béranger was Guimard's first major independent commission and the first major Art Nouveau apartment building in Paris. The building won the city's Concours de façades (best new façade competition) in 1899, establishing his reputation.

The façade demonstrates the full range of Art Nouveau architectural surface treatment: stone carvings of botanical ornament at the entrance portal; glazed ceramic tiles in asymmetric patterns on the upper floors; cast iron decorative panels; wrought iron balcony railings; coloured brick in varied bonds creating surface texture. No two bays are identical; the building grows rather than repeats.

The entrance gate — cast iron with elaborate botanical ornament — is among the most beautiful pieces of architectural metalwork of the era.

### Hôtel Guimard (Villa Guimard), Paris (1909–1913)

**Type:** Private residence (architect's own house)  
**Location:** 122 avenue Mozart, Paris 16e  
**Current status:** Private residence; listed monument

Guimard's own house, designed for himself and his wife Caroline, is the most complete surviving example of his mature domestic architecture. The building is narrower and more vertical than the Castel Béranger, with the characteristic organic window frames and stone carving, but somewhat more restrained in its ornamental program — the residence of a successful architect rather than a speculative development.

## Victor Horta (1861–1947) — Belgium

### Hôtel Tassel, Brussels (1893–1894)

**Type:** Private town house  
**Location:** 6 rue Paul-Émile Janson, Brussels  
**Materials:** Stone, iron, mosaic, stained glass, painted plaster  
**Current status:** UNESCO World Heritage Site (since 2000); private residence

The Hôtel Tassel is universally regarded as the first mature work of Art Nouveau architecture — the building that demonstrated the movement's principles could be realised in a complete, coherent architectural composition. It was commissioned by the scientist Émile Tassel as his private residence.

**The exterior:** Relatively restrained by later Art Nouveau standards; a three-bay stone façade with large windows, organic stone carving at the window surrounds, and Art Nouveau ornamental detail at the entrance. The façade's most radical feature is the open plan it reveals through the large windows — the conventional party wall between the front and rear rooms of a Brussels town house is removed, replaced by iron columns.

**The staircase hall:** The masterpiece. The central staircase rises through three floors of the building, its iron columns flowering at capital level into organic curved members that branch across the ceiling. The floor is mosaic (botanical and abstract geometric patterns in a single continuous composition); the walls are painted with botanical ornament in gold and ochre tones; the ceiling is stained glass in warm amber and gold. Every surface is part of a single unified composition in which the structural iron, the ornamental painting, the mosaic floor, and the stained glass ceiling are aspects of a single organism.

### Hôtel Solvay, Brussels (1894–1900)

**Type:** Private town house  
**Location:** 224 avenue Louise, Brussels  
**Current status:** UNESCO World Heritage Site; private property, limited public access

Horta's most elaborate and most complete work, commissioned by the industrialist Armand Solvay. The Hôtel Solvay is the fullest realisation of the Art Nouveau Gesamtkunstwerk in architecture: every element — from the iron and stone façade to the interior plasterwork, furniture, light fittings, door handles, and tableware — was designed by Horta as a unified whole.

The façade is more elaborate than the Tassel: the iron structural frame is more visible externally, with the bowed front windows and the curving iron balustrade of the main floor creating a rhythm of organic projection and recession. The entrance portal is a masterpiece of carved stone and decorative iron.

### Hôtel van Eetvelde, Brussels (1895)

**Type:** Private town house  
**Location:** 2–4 avenue Palmerston, Brussels  
**Current status:** UNESCO World Heritage Site; private offices

Commissioned by Edmond van Eetvelde, an important Belgian colonial official, the Hôtel van Eetvelde features Horta's most elaborate centralised staircase composition: an octagonal glass-roofed atrium from which the staircase radiates, the iron and glass dome overhead bathing the interior in warm natural light.

### Maison & Atelier Horta (1898–1901)

**Type:** Architect's own residence and studio  
**Location:** 23–25 rue Américaine, Brussels  
**Current status:** UNESCO World Heritage Site; Musée Horta, open to the public

Horta's own house and studio is the most publicly accessible of his four UNESCO-listed buildings and the best starting point for understanding his work. The building demonstrates his complete design vocabulary: the double-width façade (house and studio side by side), the ingenious plan that sequences public and private spaces through a series of ornamental thresholds, and the integration of every element from the entrance gate to the bathroom fixtures.

## Antoni Gaudí (1852–1926) — Spain

### Park Güell, Barcelona (1900–1914)

**Type:** Public park (originally intended as a residential development)  
**Location:** Carmel hill, Barcelona  
**Current status:** UNESCO World Heritage Site; public park

The Park Güell was originally commissioned by Eusebi Güell as a private residential park, intended to provide an English-garden-suburb environment for wealthy Barcelonans who wanted to combine urban accessibility with natural surroundings. The development failed commercially (only two houses were ever built, of which one was purchased by Gaudí himself), but the park's communal elements — the main terrace with its serpentine bench, the hypostyle hall below, the viaducts and walkways through the hillside — constitute one of the most extraordinary designed landscapes in Europe.

**The main terrace and bench:** The undulating bench that runs the perimeter of the main terrace is entirely covered in trencadís mosaic — broken ceramic and glass fragments set in organic patterns that combine abstract geometric forms with representational elements (ceramic shards from Gaudí's contemporaries, fragments of industrial tile, coloured glass). The bench's form — S-curve in plan, ergonomically profiled cross-section — was reportedly designed with Gaudí lying on fresh concrete to determine the most comfortable reclined position.

**The hypostyle hall:** The hall below the main terrace consists of 86 Doric columns of slightly unconventional proportion, their surfaces covered in trencadís, supporting a ceiling of circular medallions in polychrome mosaic.

### Casa Batlló, Barcelona (1904–1906)

**Type:** Residential apartment building  
**Location:** 43 Passeig de Gràcia, Barcelona  
**Current status:** UNESCO World Heritage Site; museum open to the public

Gaudí transformed an existing undistinguished apartment building into one of the most extraordinary works of architecture in the world. The renovation was so complete that almost nothing of the original building is visible.

**The façade:** The building's skin is entirely ceramic — glazed tiles and trencadís covering the upper floors in a surface that shifts from deep ocean blue-green at the upper floors to pale azure at the lower floors, creating the effect of light filtering through water. The structural columns of the piano nobile are shaped as bones; the balconies are formed as skull-like masks. The roofline is covered in multicoloured ceramic scales that represent the back of a dragon or (in nationalist interpretation) the dragon of Saint George.

**The interior:** The internal light well is lined in glazed tiles that are deepest blue-green at the top (where light is strongest) and paler at the bottom, maintaining consistent light quality throughout the well. The apartment interiors use mushroom-vault ceilings, undulating plaster walls, and organic window frames that admit light while controlling solar gain.

### Casa Milà ("La Pedrera"), Barcelona (1906–1912)

**Type:** Apartment building  
**Location:** 261–265 Passeig de Gràcia, Barcelona  
**Current status:** UNESCO World Heritage Site; museum open to the public

The Casa Milà — nicknamed "La Pedrera" (the stone quarry) by hostile contemporary critics — is Gaudí's most ambitious domestic commission and his most explicitly geological work. The building's entire façade is rusticated natural stone, its surfaces undulating in continuous waves; no two window openings are identical; the floor plan has no straight walls. The roofscape — with its extraordinary sculptural groups of staircase towers, ventilation shafts, and chimneys in the form of armoured warriors — is one of the iconic sights of Barcelona.

The building was the subject of considerable controversy at the time of construction, and Gaudí's relationship with the Milà family deteriorated severely over disputes about payment; he donated his entire fee to the Sagrada Família.

### Sagrada Família, Barcelona (1882–present)

**Type:** Roman Catholic minor basilica  
**Location:** Eixample district, Barcelona  
**Current status:** UNESCO World Heritage Site; under ongoing construction, expected completion c. 2026

The Sagrada Família is simultaneously the most ambitious and the most unfinished work of Art Nouveau architecture — a building that has been under continuous construction for over 140 years, is expected to be completed only decades after its architect's death, and has been described as "the greatest building project in the world" by observers ranging from admirers to critics.

Gaudí took over the project from the original architect Francisco de Paula del Villar in 1883 and immediately transformed it from a conventional Gothic Revival design into something wholly his own. By the time of his death in 1926, the apse, the crypt, and the Nativity Façade were complete. Construction has continued from Gaudí's drawings, models, and subsequent reconstructions of his intentions.

The Nativity Façade (the east façade, built during Gaudí's lifetime) is the most organically elaborate element: four spires covered in trencadís, with the façade surfaces dissolving into an extraordinary proliferation of organic sculptural detail — flora, fauna, human figures, architectural elements — in which the boundary between structure and decoration is completely dissolved.

## Otto Wagner (1841–1918) — Austria

### Vienna Stadtbahn Stations and Viaducts (1894–1901)

**Type:** Urban railway infrastructure  
**Location:** Vienna ring routes; surviving stations at Hietzing, Schönbrunn, Kettenbrückengasse, and others  
**Current status:** Many stations still in use as Vienna U-Bahn stations; others repurposed

Wagner's designs for the Vienna urban railway system constitute the most extensive application of Art Nouveau principles to public transit infrastructure. The stations range from elaborate pavilions at major interchanges to simpler covered platforms, all sharing a consistent vocabulary of cream and green enamel cladding, metal structural elements with botanical ornament, and the integration of functional requirements (shelter, ticketing, access) with aesthetic ambition.

### Majolika Haus, Vienna (1898)

**Type:** Apartment building  
**Location:** 40 Linke Wienzeile, Vienna  
**Current status:** Protected monument; private apartments

The Majolika Haus is named for its extraordinary façade treatment: the entire surface is covered in majolica tiles (glazed ceramic tile in floral patterns) in full colour, with rose stems and flower heads covering every inch of the upper floors from windowsill to roofline. The tile treatment achieves both weatherproofing (ceramic is impervious to Vienna's climate) and ornamental richness that no painted surface could match over time.

## Charles Rennie Mackintosh (1868–1928) — Scotland

### Glasgow School of Art (1897–1909)

**Type:** Art school  
**Location:** 167 Renfrew Street, Glasgow  
**Current status:** Severely damaged by fires in 2014 and 2018; restoration ongoing

The Glasgow School of Art was built in two phases: the eastern half (1897–1899) and the western half (1907–1909), separated by a decade in which Mackintosh's style evolved significantly. The building is widely regarded as one of the finest works of Art Nouveau architecture and as one of the most important works of British architecture of the twentieth century.

The north façade — facing the street and providing the primary public face — is dominated by large studio windows in bold iron frames, with concentrated organic ornamental detail at the entrance portal and on the ironwork railings. The interior spaces — particularly the library of the second phase (before the fires) — achieved an effect of extraordinary spatial richness through the integration of structural timberwork, fitted furniture, and controlled natural light.

The 2018 fire, which followed a first fire in 2014, severely damaged the western half of the building; full restoration is projected over multiple decades.

### Hill House, Helensburgh (1902–1904)

**Type:** Private residence  
**Location:** Upper Colquhoun Street, Helensburgh, Scotland  
**Current status:** National Trust for Scotland; open to the public

Hill House, designed for the publisher Walter Blackie, is the most complete surviving realisation of the Glasgow Style in a domestic setting. Mackintosh designed every element of the house — from the architecture to the furniture, textiles, cutlery, and light fittings — creating the total design environment that Art Nouveau's Gesamtkunstwerk ideal demanded.

The exterior is relatively austere — harled (roughcast) masonry, regular windows, a somewhat Scottish vernacular character — masking the exceptional interior. The master bedroom is particularly celebrated: a white room in which every element, from the fitted wardrobes to the decorative stencils on the walls, is part of a single unified composition in white, grey, and lilac.

## Other Key Buildings

### Paul Hankar — Maison Hankar, Brussels (1893)

**Location:** 71 rue Defacqz, Brussels  
**Current status:** Listed monument; private residence

Hankar's own house, contemporary with Horta's Hôtel Tassel, demonstrates a slightly different approach to the Brussels variant of Art Nouveau: more polychrome, with sgraffito panels on the façade (decorative plaster panels, incised while the plaster is still wet, revealing coloured layers beneath), and more obviously picturesque in its compositional approach.

### Ernesto Basile — Villino Florio, Palermo (1899–1903)

**Location:** Via dei Villini, Palermo, Sicily  
**Current status:** Private residence; interior altered

The Villino Florio is the finest expression of the Italian *Stile Liberty* in architectural form: a compact suburban villa whose exterior surfaces are covered in naturalistic floral carved stone, with every element of window, balcony, and doorway enriched with botanical ornament. The building is a direct translation of the French and Belgian Art Nouveau vocabulary into a Mediterranean climate and a local stone (golden Sicilian limestone) that gives it a warmth and richness distinct from the grey stone buildings of Brussels and Paris.

### Henry van de Velde — Folkwang Museum Hagen (1902)

**Location:** Hagen, Germany  
**Current status:** Demolished 1929; rebuilt in different form

Van de Velde's museum for the collector Karl Ernst Osthaus was among the most complete Art Nouveau museum interiors, with Van de Velde designing every element from the exhibition hall proportions to the display furniture and the staircase railing. The renovation of the Hohenhof (Osthaus's own residence, also by Van de Velde, 1906–1907) partially survives.
