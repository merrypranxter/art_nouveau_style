# Art Nouveau: Key Figures

## Overview

Art Nouveau was made by individuals — artists and designers whose specific temperaments, skills, and circumstances produced the works that define the movement. This document provides biographical summaries, key works, and distinctive style notes for the fifteen most important practitioners.

---

## Alphonse Mucha (1860–1939)

### Biography

Born in Ivančice in Moravia (then the Austrian Empire, now the Czech Republic), Mucha trained in Vienna and Munich before settling in Paris in 1887. His career-defining moment came in December 1894 when, as a relatively obscure commercial illustrator, he received the emergency commission for Sarah Bernhardt's *Gismonda* poster. The extraordinary result made him overnight the most sought-after poster artist in Paris and initiated a collaboration with Bernhardt that lasted until 1900.

The next decade — 1895 to 1904 — was his period of maximum commercial production: theatrical posters for Bernhardt; commercial posters for *Job* cigarette papers, Monaco, Nestlé, Moët & Chandon, and dozens of other clients; decorative panel series (*The Seasons*, *The Times of Day*, *The Moon and Stars*, *The Flowers*); jewellery and interior designs. In 1904 he emigrated to the United States, where he taught at the Art Institute of Chicago and the New York School of Art. In 1910 he returned permanently to Prague to begin his great national project: *The Slav Epic*, a cycle of twenty enormous paintings depicting the history of the Slavic peoples.

Mucha died in Prague in 1939, weeks after the Nazi occupation; he was briefly interrogated by the Gestapo and died of pneumonia in July.

### Key Works

- *Gismonda* (1894) — theatrical poster; the work that made his name
- *Job* cigarette papers poster (1896)
- *The Seasons* (1896, 1900) — two decorative panel series
- *Monaco-Monte Carlo* (1897) — travel poster
- *Médée* (1898) — theatrical poster for Bernhardt
- *Documents Décoratifs* (1902) — portfolio of design resources
- *Figures Décoratives* (1905) — further design portfolio
- *The Slav Epic* (1910–1928) — twenty monumental paintings

### Distinctive Style

Mucha's style is the most complete and systematic expression of Art Nouveau's graphic language. Its characteristics:

- **The tall narrow format:** Standard theatrical poster proportions, approximately 3:1 height-to-width, accommodating the full-length female figure
- **The circular or arched halo:** Consistently placed behind the figure's head, creating a focal point and sacred-secular ambiguity
- **The warm gold-cream palette:** Antique gold, warm cream, dusty rose, muted sage — always muted, never fully saturated
- **Botanical ornament as environment:** The figure is embedded in a botanical world rather than placed in front of a background
- **Text as integrated cartouche:** Title and secondary text always in ornamental containers consistent with the botanical vocabulary

## Gustav Klimt (1862–1918)

### Biography

Klimt was born in Baumgarten, near Vienna, the second of seven children of a gold engraver. He trained at the Vienna Kunstgewerbeschule (School of Arts and Crafts) and established himself in the 1880s as a highly successful academic decorative painter, executing large ceiling and wall paintings for Viennese public buildings. His turn away from academic convention — marked by the Vienna Secession's founding in 1897 — was swift and irreversible.

In 1897 Klimt founded the Vienna Secession with Josef Hoffmann, Koloman Moser, Joseph Maria Olbrich, and others, serving as its first president. The Secession provided the institutional framework for his mature work — including the *Beethoven Frieze* (1902), exhibited in the Secession building as part of the Beethoven Exhibition, and the commission for ceiling paintings at Vienna University that generated the "Scandal of the Faculty Paintings" (1900–1907), with their explicit nudity and disturbing symbolic content.

Klimt's late portraits and allegorical works — *The Kiss*, the Bloch-Bauer portrait, the series of allegorical landscapes — are among the canonical works of European modernism.

### Key Works

- *Judith I* (1901) — Symbolist female figure with gold-leaf landscape
- *Beethoven Frieze* (1902) — wall-length painted frieze; Belvedere Vienna
- *Portrait of Adele Bloch-Bauer I* ("Woman in Gold") (1907) — Neue Galerie, New York
- *The Kiss* (1907–1908) — Belvedere Vienna
- *Judith II (Salome)* (1909)
- *The Virgins* (1913)
- Palais Stoclet mosaic friezes (1905–1911) — executed by the Wiener Werkstätte

### Distinctive Style

Klimt's style integrates Symbolist figurative painting with Byzantine goldsmithing and Vienna Secession decorative design:

- **Gold leaf surfaces:** The application of actual gold leaf to the painting surface, creating a physical as well as visual richness
- **Figure-ground absorption:** Figures are progressively absorbed into the decorative surface; in mature works, only face and hands remain anatomically distinct from the surrounding gold pattern
- **Byzantine references:** The mosaic-like quality of the gold surface, the frontally positioned figures, the abstraction of the body into decorative geometry, all reference Byzantine icon tradition
- **Black-and-white pattern contrasts:** Strong areas of black-and-white geometric pattern punctuate the gold surfaces

## Aubrey Beardsley (1872–1898)

### Biography

Beardsley was born in Brighton and was largely self-taught as an artist, though he studied briefly at Westminster School of Art. He was a child prodigy who died of tuberculosis at 25, but in the brief five or six years of his maturity produced a body of work of startling originality and technical brilliance.

He came to attention in 1893 when his illustrations for Malory's *Le Morte d'Arthur* were published by Dent, and burst into international fame with his *Salome* illustrations in 1894. He was appointed art editor of *The Yellow Book* (1894) and later *The Savoy* magazine (1896), and in both roles placed himself at the centre of the 1890s Aesthetic movement. He converted to Roman Catholicism shortly before his death in 1898.

### Key Works

- *Salome* illustrations (1894) — fifteen designs for Wilde's play
- *Yellow Book* covers and illustrations (1894–1895)
- *The Savoy* illustrations (1896)
- *Under the Hill* illustrations (1896–1897)
- *Lysistrata* illustrations (1896) — privately printed; highly erotic
- *The Rape of the Lock* illustrations (1896) — Rococo-influenced

### Distinctive Style

Beardsley's black-and-white line work has no parallel in the Art Nouveau tradition:

- **Pure black and white:** No intermediate grey tones; forms defined entirely by the relationship of black areas to white areas
- **Extreme precision:** Achieved with a very fine pen nib; line quality suggesting both calligraphic fluency and mechanical exactitude
- **Flat black areas:** Large areas of pure black (hair, costume, shadow) function as positive design elements
- **Decorative patterning:** Intricate dot-work, stylised floral patterns, candle-flame motifs, and peacock feather patterns fill areas that in other artists' work would be left blank
- **Decadent wit:** An element of dark humour and provocative irony distinguishes his work from the earnest beauty of Mucha

## Henri de Toulouse-Lautrec (1864–1901)

### Biography

Toulouse-Lautrec was born in Albi into one of France's oldest aristocratic families. Two childhood leg fractures, resulting from a hereditary condition, left him permanently disabled and with legs that stopped growing at normal proportions. He moved to Paris in 1882, trained in academic studios, and by 1887 had established himself in Montmartre, where he became the principal visual chronicler of the café-concert, music hall, and cabaret world.

His poster work — particularly his designs for the Moulin Rouge and for individual performers — transformed poster art from mere commercial ephemera into an acknowledged fine art medium. He was prolific despite his short life, producing over 700 paintings, 5,000 drawings, and 368 lithographic prints.

### Key Works

- *Moulin Rouge: La Goulue* (1891) — the work that made poster art an art form
- *Jane Avril* series (1893–1899) — four major posters
- *Aristide Bruant dans son cabaret* (1892)
- *Divan Japonais* (1893)
- *Reine de Joie* (1892)
- *La Goulue et sa sœur* (1892)

### Distinctive Style

Toulouse-Lautrec's style is the most directly Japanese of all Art Nouveau poster artists:

- **Radical compositional simplification:** Figures reduced to their essential silhouette; backgrounds eliminated or reduced to flat colour
- **Japanese cropping:** Figures cut off at the edge of the frame, seen from unusual angles
- **The telling detail:** Character conveyed through a single specific gesture or expression rather than through comprehensive description
- **Satirical edge:** Unlike Mucha's idealising tendency, Toulouse-Lautrec's subjects are always specific individuals, observed with affection but without idealisation

## Émile Gallé (1846–1904)

### Biography

Born in Nancy, Gallé inherited his father's glassware and ceramics business and transformed it into the most celebrated glass studio in Art Nouveau history. He was simultaneously an accomplished botanist, a poet (verses were sometimes engraved on his pieces), and a political figure — his public support for Alfred Dreyfus made him a significant voice in the Dreyfus Affair.

He founded the École de Nancy in 1901 and died of leukemia in 1904. His workshop continued under the Gallé brand until 1931.

### Key Works

- Cameo glass vases with iris, orchid, and dragonfly motifs (c. 1895–1904)
- *Orpheus* vase (c. 1900) — with *marqueterie de verre* technique
- Mushroom-lamp series (c. 1900–1904)
- Furniture with botanical marquetry (c. 1900–1904)

### Distinctive Style

- **Multi-layer cameo glass:** Three or more glass layers in contrasting colours, acid-etched to reveal botanical compositions in relief
- **Naturalistic botanical accuracy:** Flowers, insects, and plants rendered with scientific correctness, reflecting his botanical training
- **Literary glass:** Verses from Verlaine, Baudelaire, and others engraved on pieces; art and poetry integrated
- **Marqueterie de verre:** Unique fused-surface technique creating embedded colour effects

## Louis Comfort Tiffany (1848–1933)

### Biography

Born in New York, the son of the Tiffany & Co. jewellery founder, Tiffany studied painting in New York and Paris and was initially known as a painter. In the 1870s he turned decisively toward decorative arts, founding Tiffany Glass and Decorating Company in 1893 (reorganised as Tiffany Studios in 1902). He served as artistic director of the company until 1933.

Tiffany's success was extraordinary: by 1900 his studios employed approximately 300 people and had executed commissions for the White House, numerous churches, and wealthy private clients throughout the United States and Europe.

### Key Works

- *Wisteria* lamp (1902) — the most complex leaded glass lamp ever made
- Dragonfly lamp series (various dates, c. 1900–1910)
- *Peony* lamp (c. 1900)
- *Magnolia* window series (various commissions)
- Favrile glass vases (1893–1933)

### Distinctive Style

- **Copper-foil technique:** Allowing unprecedented complexity in leaded glass composition
- **Favrile glass:** Iridescent, internally varied glass with metallic-oxide surface treatment
- **Nature as structural model:** Lamp forms derived from plant structures; shades designed as flowers, mushrooms, dragonfly clouds
- **American optimism:** A sunnier, less melancholy version of Art Nouveau than the French mainstream; more concerned with beauty than with Symbolist darkness

## Hector Guimard (1867–1942)

### Biography

Born in Lyon, Guimard trained at the École des Arts Décoratifs and the École des Beaux-Arts in Paris. A visit to Brussels in 1895, where he studied Victor Horta's Hôtel Tassel, was decisive in directing him toward organic architecture. His commission to design the entrances for the new Paris Métro (1900) gave him the opportunity to express Art Nouveau principles on the most public possible scale.

Guimard designed a range of building types — apartment buildings, villas, a concert hall (the Humbert de Romans Auditorium, 1901, demolished 1905) — all in his distinctive organic style. He emigrated to the United States in 1938, following the German occupation of France, and died in New York in 1942.

### Key Works

- Paris Métro entrance edicules (1900–1913) — cast iron, original green colour
- Castel Béranger, Paris (1898) — first Art Nouveau residential building in Paris
- Hôtel Guimard (1909–1913) — his own Paris residence
- Villa La Bluette, Hermanville-sur-Mer (1898)

### Distinctive Style

- **Organic iron forms:** Cast iron structural members that curve, taper, and flower into organic terminal forms
- **Asymmetric composition:** No two sides of a building or entrance treated identically
- **Ceramic ornament:** Richly coloured glazed tiles embedded in façades
- **The "Style Guimard":** Recognisable at a glance — no other architect in the world worked in this visual language

## Victor Horta (1861–1947)

### Biography

Born in Ghent, Horta trained at the Ghent Academy and subsequently in Paris before settling permanently in Brussels. His four major town house commissions of the 1890s — Hôtel Tassel, Hôtel Solvay, Hôtel van Eetvelde, and his own Maison & Atelier Horta — represent the highest achievement of Art Nouveau residential architecture.

In later life Horta moved away from Art Nouveau toward a stripped classical modernism, designing the Palais des Beaux-Arts in Brussels (1928) — a building in which nothing of his Art Nouveau manner survives. He served as director of the Brussels Académie des Beaux-Arts from 1927 to 1931.

### Key Works

- Hôtel Tassel, Brussels (1893) — the first mature Art Nouveau building
- Hôtel Solvay, Brussels (1894–1900) — the most complete Art Nouveau Gesamtkunstwerk in architecture
- Hôtel van Eetvelde, Brussels (1895)
- Maison & Atelier Horta (1898–1901) — now the Horta Museum

### Distinctive Style

- **Iron as organic expression:** The structural iron members of staircases and columns are allowed to curve and branch into vine-like forms, celebrating their material properties rather than concealing them
- **Total design:** Every element of interior — floor mosaic, stained glass, iron railing, painted wall, light fitting, door hardware — conceived as part of a unified visual system
- **The staircase as masterpiece:** In each of his major houses, the staircase hall is the spatial and decorative climax of the design

## Antoni Gaudí (1852–1926)

### Biography

Born in Reus, Catalonia, Gaudí studied architecture in Barcelona and quickly came under the influence of the industrial magnate and art patron Eusebi Güell, who became his primary patron for much of his career. Gaudí was deeply Catholic — in his later years essentially a religious mystic — and the Sagrada Família basilica, which he took over in 1883 and on which he worked exclusively from 1914 until his death, was the expression of his complete artistic and spiritual ambition.

He died when struck by a tram in Barcelona in June 1926. He was initially unrecognised and was nearly taken to a pauper's hospital; when his identity was established, his funeral was attended by enormous crowds.

### Key Works

- Casa Vicens, Barcelona (1883–1888) — early Moorish-influenced work
- Palau Güell, Barcelona (1886–1890) — first major commission for Güell
- Park Güell, Barcelona (1900–1914) — landscaped public park with trencadís benches and terraces
- Casa Batlló, Barcelona (1904–1906) — radical organic transformation of existing building
- Casa Milà ("La Pedrera"), Barcelona (1906–1912) — undulating stone façade building
- Sagrada Família, Barcelona (1882–present, under ongoing construction) — his life's work

### Distinctive Style

Gaudí transcended Art Nouveau categorisation to create a wholly individual organic architecture:

- **Structural catenary logic:** Forms derived from the natural mechanics of hanging chains and suspended loads, translated into stone and concrete structures that arch and vault without conventional buttressing
- **Trencadís mosaic:** Broken ceramic and glass mosaic on all curved surfaces, allowing colour across complex three-dimensional geometry
- **Marine and geological references:** Building surfaces that recall geological formations, cave interiors, and marine organisms rather than historical architectural orders
- **Sacred geometry:** In mature religious works, elaborate symbolic geometry derived from Gothic structural logic combined with organic natural forms

## Charles Rennie Mackintosh (1868–1928)

### Biography

Born in Glasgow, Mackintosh joined the architectural firm Honeyman & Keppie as a draughtsman in 1889, becoming a partner in 1901. His four years of night study at the Glasgow School of Art brought him into contact with Margaret Macdonald, Herbert MacNair, and Frances Macdonald — together forming the "Glasgow Four" whose distinctive rectilinear Art Nouveau style was admired throughout Europe, particularly in Vienna.

Mackintosh's career was marked by persistent commercial failure alongside great critical esteem. After his most important commissions — the Glasgow School of Art (1897–1909) and Hill House (1902) — his practice declined. He retired to Port-Vendres in southern France in 1923, where he painted watercolours until his death in 1928.

### Key Works

- Glasgow School of Art (1897–1909) — considered among the finest buildings of the Art Nouveau era
- Hill House, Helensburgh (1902) — most complete domestic Gesamtkunstwerk in Scotland
- Willow Tea Rooms, Glasgow (1903) — surviving public interior
- Scotland Street School, Glasgow (1903–1906)

### Distinctive Style

The Glasgow Style is the most rectilinear and austere of the Art Nouveau variants:

- **Black-white-grey palette** with muted lilac and rose accents; the most restrained of all Art Nouveau regional palettes
- **Tall, narrow proportions:** Elongated figures, attenuated column forms, high-back chairs
- **The Glasgow rose:** A stylised, highly abstracted rose motif used as a primary ornamental element
- **Japanese space:** Interior spaces organised on principles of Japanese aesthetic austerity — the controlled arrangement of empty space as an active design element

## Margaret Macdonald (1864–1933)

### Biography

Born in Wolverhampton, Margaret Macdonald trained at the Glasgow School of Art alongside Frances Macdonald, Herbert MacNair, and Charles Rennie Mackintosh, whom she married in 1900. The Glasgow Four exhibited together in London (1896) and Vienna (1900), where their work was received with exceptional enthusiasm by the Vienna Secession; Klimt reportedly said "There is no Margaret Macdonald in any of us."

Margaret's contribution to the Glasgow Style — in gesso panels, painted panels, textile work, and metalwork — was essential to its distinctive character. Klimt's mature gold-surface style shows direct influence from her ethereal, attenuated figures.

### Key Works

- *La Mort Parfumée* (1921) — major gesso panel
- Hill House decorative panels (1902) — designed in collaboration with Mackintosh
- *The Opera of the Winds* (1903)

## Louis Majorelle (1859–1926)

### Biography

Born in Toul, Majorelle inherited his father's faïence and furniture workshop in Nancy and transformed it into one of the most important Art Nouveau furniture studios. He was a founding member of the École de Nancy (1901) alongside Gallé.

### Distinctive Style

Majorelle's furniture is characterised by the literal rendering of botanical forms as structural elements:
- Chair legs shaped as lily stems, ending in calyx-form feet
- Cabinet doors framed by carved relief of orchid, water lily, or iris
- Bronze hardware mounts cast as seed pods, buds, and dragonflies
- Rich wood surfaces combining multiple species in elaborate marquetry

## René Lalique (1860–1945)

### Biography

Lalique was born in Ay, Marne, and trained as a jeweller in Paris and London before establishing his own studio in 1885. His exhibition at the 1900 Paris Exposition Universelle made him internationally famous; his technical innovations in jewellery — particularly the use of plique-à-jour enamel, horn, ivory, and enamel painting — transformed the art.

In the 1920s Lalique transitioned from jewellery to glass production, becoming one of the most important Art Deco glass designers. The Lalique company continues to operate as a luxury glass and crystal manufacturer.

### Key Works

- Dragonfly corsage ornament (c. 1897–1898) — Calouste Gulbenkian Museum, Lisbon
- *Femme libellule* (Dragonfly Woman) brooch (c. 1897–1898)
- *Orchid* corsage ornament series (c. 1900)
- Numerous comb, hair ornament, and brooch designs

### Distinctive Style

- **The living jewel:** The jewel as an organism rather than a precious container; the metal and enamel as the primary material, the gemstones as accent only
- **Plique-à-jour excellence:** The most accomplished practitioner of this technique in Art Nouveau history
- **Insect and botanical fusion:** Dragonfly, moth, beetle, and orchid forms combined with female figures into single unified jewel-organisms

## Jan Toorop (1858–1928)

### Biography

Born in Poerworedjö, Java (then Dutch East Indies), Toorop was of mixed Dutch-Javanese descent. He studied in Amsterdam, Brussels, and London, and was associated with the Belgian avant-garde in the 1880s before developing his mature Symbolist-Art Nouveau style in the 1890s. His Javanese heritage gave his work a distinctive quality — the extraordinarily elongated, sinuous figures and the dense linear patterning of his most celebrated works show the influence of Javanese shadow puppet (*wayang*) art alongside the European Art Nouveau tradition.

### Key Works

- *The Three Brides* (1893) — most celebrated Symbolist-Art Nouveau work
- *Fatalism* (1893)
- Poster for Delftsche Slaolie (1895) — among the finest Art Nouveau posters
