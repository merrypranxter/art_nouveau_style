# Art Nouveau: Chronological Timeline

## Overview

This timeline traces Art Nouveau from its earliest precedents in the 1840s through its apex at the 1900 Paris Exposition Universelle, its wartime eclipse, its postwar dismissal, and its remarkable revival in the counterculture of the 1960s and subsequent decades.

---

## Pre-History: Seeds of the Movement (1840s–1888)

### 1848 — Pre-Raphaelite Brotherhood Founded
Dante Gabriel Rossetti, William Holman Hunt, and John Everett Millais form the Pre-Raphaelite Brotherhood in London. Their commitment to flat, jewel-like colour, medieval subject matter, and the idealised feminine figure will provide Art Nouveau with some of its most persistent themes. Edward Burne-Jones, a later affiliate, becomes one of the movement's most direct pictorial ancestors.

### 1851 — The Great Exhibition, Crystal Palace, London
The international exhibition at the Crystal Palace reveals the poverty of mass-produced industrial ornament. The reaction among critics — including Henry Cole, Owen Jones, and eventually John Ruskin — generates the reform tradition that will eventually produce Arts and Crafts and, through it, Art Nouveau.

### 1856 — Owen Jones, *The Grammar of Ornament*
Owen Jones publishes his encyclopaedic survey of historical ornament, with particular attention to Islamic, Indian, and Celtic design. His principle that ornament must grow from and express the structure of the object it decorates becomes foundational for the entire reformist design tradition.

### 1858 — Opening of Japan to Western Trade (ongoing effects)
The forced opening of Japan to Western commerce through the Convention of Kanagawa (1854) begins flooding European markets with Japanese woodblock prints, textiles, lacquerwork, ceramics, and metalwork. The Japonisme craze builds through the 1860s and 1870s, with decisive influence on the decorative language of Art Nouveau.

### 1861 — Morris, Marshall, Faulkner & Co. Founded
William Morris founds his firm in London, producing wallpapers, textiles, tiles, stained glass, and furniture based on the principle that useful objects must be beautiful, and beauty must be grounded in honest craftsmanship. The firm becomes the model for Arts and Crafts production and a decisive influence on Art Nouveau's ambitions.

### 1862 — International Exhibition, London
Japanese objects are displayed to a wide European audience for the first time at the South Kensington International Exhibition. Artists including James McNeill Whistler and Edward William Godwin are profoundly affected.

### 1874 — First Impressionist Exhibition, Paris
The Impressionists' break with academic tradition — their rejection of conventional finish, their interest in light and atmosphere, their informal compositions — creates the cultural permission for subsequent avant-garde movements to reject historicism and seek new visual languages.

### 1876 — Siegfried Bing Opens Japanese Art Gallery, Paris
Bing opens "La Maison de l'Art Japonais" in Paris, becoming the principal commercial conduit for Japanese art into the French market. His clients include Edgar Degas, Claude Monet, Mary Cassatt, and virtually every artist who will later contribute to Art Nouveau.

### 1880 — *Le Japonisme* and Artistic Journals
European illustrated journals begin systematically reproducing Japanese prints alongside European avant-garde work. The formal lessons — flat colour, asymmetric composition, decorative line — become widely available to the emerging generation of designers.

### 1883 — Hokusai's *Manga* in European Circulation
Hokusai's fifteen-volume sketchbook *Manga*, containing thousands of studies of plants, animals, human figures, and natural phenomena drawn with extraordinary economy of line, circulates widely among European artists and designers as a sourcebook of organic form.

### 1884 — Salon des Indépendants, Paris
The establishment of the Salon des Indépendants provides a major exhibition venue independent of the academic Salon, facilitating the emergence of Post-Impressionism, Symbolism, and ultimately the Art Nouveau generation.

### 1886 — The Symbolist Manifesto
Jean Moréas publishes the Symbolist manifesto in *Le Figaro*, defining a literary and artistic movement focused on inner states, suggestion, and the mystical rather than the documentary. The Symbolist atmosphere — its dreamlike femininity, its interest in the unconscious — will permeate Art Nouveau.

### 1888 — *Jugend* Precursor Publications, Munich
Munich's artistic community begins producing illustrated periodicals that will develop into the Jugendstil movement. The decorative illustrations accompanying literary texts are the direct predecessors of Art Nouveau graphic design.

## The Formation Period (1889–1895)

### 1889 — Paris Exposition Universelle
The great international exhibition of 1889, featuring Gustave Eiffel's tower, becomes a crucible for Art Nouveau ideas. The exhibition's applied arts sections display furniture, ceramics, glass, and jewellery by designers whose work is moving toward the new style. The iron structure of the Eiffel Tower itself demonstrates the architectural possibilities of the material.

### 1890 — Emile Gallé's Early Glass Work
Gallé begins producing his signature cameo glass pieces in Nancy, drawing on Japanese motifs and natural history. His work with acid-etched, wheel-carved, and cased glass will become one of Art Nouveau's most celebrated technical achievements.

### 1891 — Toulouse-Lautrec, *Moulin Rouge: La Goulue*
Henri de Toulouse-Lautrec designs his first major poster, for the Moulin Rouge music hall. Its bold flat colour, strong silhouette, Japanese compositional influence, and integration of lettering with image create the template for Art Nouveau poster design. The poster is printed in an edition of 3,000 and pasted throughout Paris.

### 1892 — The Nabis Group
Paul Sérusier, Maurice Denis, Pierre Bonnard, and Édouard Vuillard found the Nabis group, which bridges Post-Impressionist painting and applied decoration. Their interest in flat colour, decorative patterning, and the integration of art and everyday life runs parallel to Art Nouveau.

### 1893 — Victor Horta, Hôtel Tassel, Brussels
Horta completes the Hôtel Tassel, widely considered the first mature Art Nouveau building. The structural iron columns of the staircase hall are allowed to flower into organic curves; the mosaic floors, painted walls, and stained glass windows continue the vegetative motif across every surface. The building demonstrates that the new aesthetic can achieve architectural synthesis, not merely decorative addition.

### 1893 — *The Studio* Magazine Founded, London
The British art journal *The Studio* begins publication, providing a crucial vehicle for the international circulation of Art Nouveau designs, posters, and architectural images. It publishes work by Beardsley, Mackintosh, Voysey, and Continental designers, creating the first truly international audience for the movement.

### 1894 — Aubrey Beardsley, *Salome* Illustrations
Aubrey Beardsley completes his illustrations for Oscar Wilde's *Salome*, published by John Lane. The stark black-and-white compositions, with their flat decorative patterning, peacock feather motifs, japoniste line work, and disturbing erotic symbolism, make him instantly famous throughout Europe. Beardsley's graphic style is widely imitated in Germany, Austria, and France.

### 1894 — Mucha, *Gismonda* Poster
In December 1894, Alphonse Mucha accepts a rush commission to design a poster for Sarah Bernhardt's play *Gismonda* at the Théâtre de la Renaissance. The resulting design — a tall, narrow format with the actress in Byzantine costume, surrounded by mosaic-inspired borders and an arched top — is plastered throughout Paris on New Year's Day 1895. Bernhardt is so delighted that she signs Mucha to a six-year contract.

### 1895 — Bing Opens La Maison de l'Art Nouveau, Paris
**26 December 1895:** Siegfried Bing opens his gallery at 22 rue de Provence, Paris, under the name "La Maison de l'Art Nouveau." The opening exhibition includes Tiffany glass, Van de Velde furniture, prints by Toulouse-Lautrec and Bonnard, and decorative rooms designed by Édouard Colonna, Georges de Feure, and others. The name "Art Nouveau" begins to be used as a generic label for the emerging style.

### 1895 — *Jugend* Magazine Founded, Munich
The Munich illustrated weekly *Jugend* begins publication, quickly becoming the flagship of the German Art Nouveau movement and giving it its name: Jugendstil (Youth Style). Early issues feature decorative borders, illustrations, and cover designs that establish the distinctive German variant of the movement's visual language.

### 1895 — *Pan* Magazine Founded, Berlin
The German art journal *Pan* publishes lavish reproductions of contemporary art in a large format that allows the decorative qualities of Art Nouveau design to be seen at full scale.

## The High Period (1896–1905)

### 1896 — Mucha, *Job* Cigarette Papers Poster
Mucha designs the iconic *Job* poster for Chambon Frères cigarette papers. The image of a young woman with swirling smoke-like hair and ecstatic expression becomes one of the defining images of the era. Its intimate scale (it was reproduced on cigarette paper packaging as well as in poster format) gives it an unusual intimacy.

### 1896 — Mucha, *The Seasons* Decorative Panels (First Series)
Mucha creates his first set of decorative panels representing the four seasons, printed in colour lithography. The series establishes the format — allegorical female figures embedded in stylised botanical environments — that will make him the most reproduced artist of the era.

### 1896 — *Ver Sacrum* Journal Announced, Vienna
The Vienna Secession's journal *Ver Sacrum* ("Sacred Spring") is announced; it will begin publication in January 1898. Its integrated design, with text and image treated as a unified visual field, becomes one of the finest examples of Art Nouveau typography and book design.

### 1897 — Vienna Secession Founded
**April 1897:** Gustav Klimt, Josef Hoffmann, Koloman Moser, Joseph Maria Olbrich, and other Viennese artists resign from the conservative Künstlerhaus to found the Vienna Secession. Their motto — *Der Zeit ihre Kunst, der Kunst ihre Freiheit* ("To every age its art, to every art its freedom") — is inscribed on the Secession building.

### 1897 — Mackintosh, Glasgow School of Art Competition Win
Charles Rennie Mackintosh wins the competition to design the new Glasgow School of Art. His design, executed in two phases (1897–1899 and 1907–1909), becomes the finest example of the Glasgow Style in architecture.

### 1897 — Munich Secessionist Exhibition
The Munich Secession exhibition presents a comprehensive overview of the Jugendstil movement, with contributions from painters, graphic designers, and applied artists. The exhibition establishes Munich as a major centre of the movement alongside Paris and Brussels.

### 1898 — Guimard, Castel Béranger, Paris
Hector Guimard completes the Castel Béranger apartment building in Paris, the first major expression of Art Nouveau in Parisian residential architecture. The building's façade, with its organic ceramic tile decoration and asymmetric composition, wins the prize for the most beautiful building façade in Paris.

### 1898 — Vienna Secession Building Opens
Joseph Maria Olbrich completes the Vienna Secession building, with its famous gilded laurel-leaf dome (nicknamed "the golden cabbage" by hostile critics). The building provides the Secession with its own exhibition space, independent of all established institutions.

### 1898 — Mucha, *Monaco-Monte Carlo* Poster
Mucha designs the travel poster for the Principality of Monaco, featuring his characteristic female figure in an arched frame of stylised olive branches. The poster typifies his integration of landscape, figure, and ornamental border.

### 1899 — Lalique Exhibits Jewellery, Salon de la Société des Beaux-Arts
René Lalique exhibits his Art Nouveau jewellery — featuring dragonfly women, orchid brooches, plique-à-jour enamel — at the Paris Salon, to extraordinary critical and public success. His technique of making the human body part of the jewel rather than merely its setting transforms the history of French jewellery.

### 1900 — Paris Exposition Universelle
**April–November 1900:** The Paris World's Fair draws fifty million visitors and provides the fullest public expression of Art Nouveau. Guimard's newly installed Paris Métro entrances frame the exhibition site. National pavilions from Belgium, Germany, Austria, and Finland display the range of European variants. Tiffany glass, Gallé glass, Lalique jewellery, and Mucha's decorations for the Bosnia-Herzegovina pavilion all receive international acclaim.

### 1900 — Mucha, *The Seasons* (Second Series) and Decorative Documents
Mucha's second *Seasons* series and the first portfolio of his *Documents Décoratifs* demonstrate the artist at the height of his powers. The *Documents* are conceived as a practical resource for designers, with detailed drawings of Art Nouveau borders, strapwork, figures, and furniture.

### 1900 — Tiffany Studios, *Wisteria* Lamp
Tiffany Studios (established 1893 as Tiffany Glass and Decorating Company) produces the *Wisteria* leaded-glass lamp, one of the most complex and celebrated objects in the American Arts and Crafts tradition. The shade contains over two thousand individually shaped pieces of glass.

### 1901 — École de Nancy Founded
The Alliance Provinciale des Industries d'Art, known as the École de Nancy, is formally constituted under Gallé's presidency. The school formalises the Nancy cluster of glass, furniture, and textile designers and gives them a collective identity and exhibition programme.

### 1901 — Klimt, *Judith I*
Klimt exhibits *Judith I*, in which a sensuous female figure in a gold-leaf frame — her expression simultaneously sated, ecstatic, and predatory — embodies the Vienna Secession's synthesis of Symbolist content and decorative surface.

### 1902 — Klimt, *Beethoven Frieze*
For the Vienna Secession's Beethoven exhibition, Klimt creates a major painted frieze intended to be seen in conjunction with Max Klinger's sculpture of Beethoven. The frieze, with its elongated female figures, Byzantine gold tessellation, and integration of painting and architecture, is one of the defining works of the Gesamtkunstwerk ideal.

### 1902 — Mackintosh, Hill House, Helensburgh
Mackintosh designs Hill House for the publisher Walter Blackie — the most complete domestic expression of the Glasgow Style, in which every detail from the exterior form to the light fixtures and book bindings is treated as part of a unified whole.

### 1903 — Wiener Werkstätte Founded
Josef Hoffmann and Koloman Moser found the Wiener Werkstätte (Vienna Workshops) with the support of the industrialist Fritz Waerndorfer. The workshops produce luxury goods in metal, leather, wood, textile, and glass with an increasingly geometric aesthetic that marks a transition away from the organic curvilinearity of mainstream Art Nouveau.

### 1903 — Gaudí Begins Casa Batlló Renovation
Antoni Gaudí begins the radical transformation of an existing apartment block on the Passeig de Gràcia in Barcelona. The resulting building, completed in 1906, with its bone-like structural columns, dragon-scale roof, and entirely ceramic-clad façade, is one of the most original works of architecture of the twentieth century.

### 1904 — Gallé Dies; Nancy School Continues
Émile Gallé dies on 23 September 1904. His workshop continues under the direction of his collaborators, and Daum Frères maintains the Nancy tradition. The École de Nancy holds memorial exhibitions and continues to exhibit.

### 1905 — Vienna Secession Splits
A faction within the Vienna Secession, centred on the Klimt group, resigns in a dispute over the increasing commercialisation of the Secession's exhibitions. The split marks a significant moment in the movement's internal evolution.

### 1907 — Klimt, *The Kiss*
Klimt completes *The Kiss*, now in the Österreichische Galerie Belvedere, Vienna. The work's extraordinary gold-leaf surface, its reduction of the embracing figures to a single unified decorative form, and its integration of abstract pattern with intimate emotion make it the most universally reproduced work of the entire movement.

### 1907 — Klimt, *Portrait of Adele Bloch-Bauer I*
Klimt completes his portrait of the Viennese socialite Adele Bloch-Bauer, with its astonishing integration of the sitter's figure into a field of Byzantine gold ornament. The painting is sometimes called "The Woman in Gold."

## Transition and Decline (1908–1920)

### 1908 — Adolf Loos, "Ornament and Crime" Lecture
The Viennese architect Adolf Loos delivers (and subsequently publishes) his polemical essay "Ornament and Crime," arguing that ornament is a mark of cultural regression and that the modern spirit demands pure, undecorated form. The essay is a direct attack on everything Art Nouveau represents, and its influence on the next generation of designers is profound.

### 1909 — Futurist Manifesto
Filippo Marinetti publishes the Futurist Manifesto, celebrating speed, machinery, and violence and explicitly rejecting the past. The manifesto's aggressive modernity makes Art Nouveau's organic romanticism seem nostalgic and backward-looking by contrast.

### 1910 — Mucha Returns to Prague
After sixteen years in Paris, Mucha returns to his native Bohemia and begins work on the Slav Epic cycle, a series of twenty enormous paintings depicting the history of the Slavic peoples. The shift from decorative graphics to monumental historical painting marks a change in his priorities that accompanies a broader cultural shift.

### 1912–1914 — Art Nouveau's Last Major Commissions
The Palais Stoclet in Brussels (designed by Josef Hoffmann, with Klimt mosaics in the dining room) is completed in 1911. Major Art Nouveau architectural projects are increasingly rare as commissions shift toward more austere and functional approaches.

### 1914 — Outbreak of the First World War
**4 August 1914:** Britain declares war on Germany. The First World War effectively ends the Art Nouveau era. The international community of designers and artists is severed by national conflict; the Belle Époque culture that had sustained the movement collapses; and the war's industrial carnage makes the movement's organic romanticism seem irrelevant or obscene.

## Decline and Displacement (1915–1930)

### 1917 — De Stijl Founded, Netherlands
Theo van Doesburg, Piet Mondrian, and others found the De Stijl movement, with its radical reduction of art to primary colours and horizontal-vertical geometry. The movement is a direct negation of Art Nouveau's organic complexity.

### 1919 — Bauhaus Founded, Weimar
Walter Gropius founds the Bauhaus in Weimar, Germany. The school's commitment to functional design, industrial production methods, and abstract geometric form makes it the most influential institutional statement of everything that Art Nouveau is not. Bauhaus graduates and teachers systematically replace Art Nouveau as the dominant influence in European design education.

### 1920s — Art Deco Displaces Art Nouveau
The style that would be retrospectively named Art Deco begins to dominate Parisian fashion and design from around 1910 but reaches full public visibility in the 1920s. The 1925 Exposition Internationale des Arts Décoratifs et Industriels Modernes, which gives Art Deco its name, presents a vision of modernity that retains decorative ambition but replaces organic curves with geometric abstraction, sunburst patterns, and streamlined symmetry.

### 1925 — Exposition des Arts Décoratifs, Paris
The 1925 Paris Exposition definitively marks Art Nouveau as a historical style. Works in the Art Nouveau manner are absent; the exhibited objects speak the language of geometric modernism. Critics begin writing of Art Nouveau in the past tense.

### 1926 — Gaudí Dies
Antoni Gaudí is killed by a tram in Barcelona on 10 June 1926. His death is mourned as the end of an era in Catalan architecture.

### 1930s — Art Nouveau Actively Derided
In the 1930s and 1940s, Art Nouveau is widely dismissed by modernist critics as decadent, bourgeois, over-ornamented, and historically retrograde. Buildings are demolished; decorative objects are melted down or destroyed. The Guimard Métro entrances survive only because they are cast iron and replacement would be expensive.

## Revival and Renaissance (1960s–Present)

### 1960 — Museum of Modern Art Exhibition: Art Nouveau
The Museum of Modern Art in New York holds a major Art Nouveau exhibition curated by Peter Selz and Mildred Constantine. The exhibition rehabilitates the movement as a serious subject of art-historical and design study, and its catalogue becomes widely influential.

### 1960s — Counterculture and Psychedelic Poster Art
The American counterculture of the 1960s — centred in San Francisco's Haight-Ashbury district — discovers Art Nouveau as a visual vocabulary perfectly suited to its rejection of mainstream modernity. Psychedelic rock concert posters by Victor Moscoso, Wes Wilson, Rick Griffin, Alton Kelley, and Stanley Mouse adopt Art Nouveau's curvilinear typography, sinuous female figures, and decorative borders. The influence is direct: Moscoso had studied colour theory and was aware of Jugendstil lettering; Wilson explicitly acknowledged Mucha and Eckmann.

### 1966 — *Nouveau Art Nouveau* Exhibition, Brussels
A major retrospective at the Musée des Arts Décoratifs in Brussels, organised in conjunction with the 1966 Exposition Universelle, introduces a new European generation to the movement.

### 1968 — Wes Wilson and the Art Nouveau Poster Revival
Wes Wilson designs concert posters for the Fillmore Auditorium in San Francisco using lettering directly derived from Alfred Roller's Vienna Secession posters of 1902. The sinuous, nearly illegible lettering becomes the defining visual signature of the psychedelic era.

### 1970s — Academic Rehabilitation and Museum Collecting
Art Nouveau objects begin to be actively collected by major museums and private collectors. Prices for Tiffany lamps, Gallé glass, Mucha posters, and Lalique jewellery rise dramatically. Scholarly monographs by Robert Schmutzler (*Art Nouveau*, 1962) and Stephan Tschudi Madsen (*Sources of Art Nouveau*, 1956) establish the movement's art-historical framework.

### 1980s–1990s — Commercial Revival
Art Nouveau imagery re-enters commercial culture through advertising, packaging, and fashion. Mucha's designs are reproduced on posters, greeting cards, and calendars with global distribution. The sinuous female figure and the decorative botanical border become shorthand for "fin de siècle" elegance.

### 1994 — Mucha Foundation Established, Prague
The Mucha Foundation is established by the artist's grandson, John Mucha, to preserve and promote the artist's legacy. The Mucha Museum opens in Prague in 1998.

### 2000s — Digital Art Nouveau Renaissance
The development of digital illustration tools (particularly Adobe Illustrator and Photoshop) enables a new generation of artists to work in Art Nouveau-influenced styles with unprecedented precision. Neo-Art Nouveau illustration becomes a thriving niche within digital art communities.

### 2010s — Tattoo Art and Fine Jewelry Revival
Art Nouveau motifs — flowing botanical forms, sinuous female figures, dragonflies, peacocks — become among the most popular subjects in contemporary fine-line tattoo art. A new generation of jewellers revives plique-à-jour enamel, filigree, and insect/botanical motifs in handcrafted pieces.

### 2024–Present — Ongoing Digital and Fashion Renaissance
Art Nouveau influences continue to permeate luxury branding, fragrance packaging, digital ornamental design, video game art direction, and fashion (particularly in collections engaging with historical costume and nature themes).
